William Thomas Kwiatkowski (May 17, 1919 - May 25, 2025) was an American military officer, politician, spy and amateur football player of Polish descent, that served as the President of the United States between 2001 and 2009. He fought in World War II, Korea and Vietnam, eventually achieving the four-star rank of General and winning five medals of honor. He also served as the Mayor of Phoenix (1981-1989), the Governor of Arizona (1989-1997) and the director of the independent intelligence agency Secret Operations Department (2010-2025)

Early life and education[]

William Kwiatkowski was born in Phoenix, Arizona, the youngest of the 6 children of lawyer Cezary Kwiatkowski (1884-1971) and nurse Mary Grace Kwiatkowski (1884-1974), née Carter. They also had a doberman pinscher called Snoopy, who coincidentally shared the same name of the future famous fictional beagle. He grew up in the family's acreage outside Phoenix, and since an early age he was instilled strong moral values of justice and patriotism. William was also the grandson of Polish con-artist-turned-soldier Jakub Kwiatkowski.

In 1924, he attended St. Mary’s Primary School, an all-boys school, and became friends with Latrell Sellers. As he grew up, he showed exceptional abilities in sports, especially football, a passion that his family always encouraged. However, young William also enjoyed reading, particularly his father’s collection of military history enciclopedias, which began his interest for everything related to war and the Army. In 1930, he was enrolled in Nate Hole Middle School, and joined the football team, soon standing out as one of the best players.

In 1933, he started high school at Phoenix High School, where he also played in the football team, becoming its captain at the age of 17. He also received the best grades in his class, excelling in history and PE.

In 1937, after graduating valedictorian, he received a full athletic scholarship to go to Arizona University, and began a degree in Political Science. William was one of the best fullbacks in the country, becoming captain of the university’s team in 1940. However, it was here that his true interest in the military began, leading him to join the university's ROTC (Reserve Officers' Training Corps). He also rushed the Alpha Sigma Phi fraternity.

Military career[]

World War II[]

After Kwiatkowski graduated with honors, he became eligible for the 1941 NFL draft, but that same year, the Japanese attacked Pearl Harbor and he decided to join the army. He was commissioned a second lieutenant and assigned to the 9th Infantry Division, and fought in Operation Torch, Tunisia and Sicily. In 1944, he participated in the invasion of Normandy, being deployed in Omaha Beach, where he earned his first medal of honor and silver star for his outstanding bravery. Many years later, in 1955, he would be awarded another medal of honor in recognition of his actions during the Battle of the Bulge. He finished the war as a captain.

Korean War[]

Recently promoted to Major, Kwiatkowski returned to active duty in 1950 after the outbreak Korean War, commanding a battalion in the 7th Infantry Division. His most important action was in the Battle of Heartbreak Ridge in 1951, where he received his second medal of honor, as well as a purple heart.

Vietnam War[]

In 1963, he was promoted to Colonel and travelled to Vietnam as a military advisor for the South Vietnamese Army. In 1965, when the US officially entered the war, Kwiatkowski was given command of the MACV-SOG, a highly classified special operations unit, and later of the II Field Force, the largest corps command in Vietnam. In 1969, he was appointed by President Lyndon B. Johnson commander of the Military Assistance Command, Vietnam (MACV).

Post-war and retirement[]

After the withdrawal of US troops from Vietnam in 1973, he became Vice Chief of Staff of the Army in the Pentagon, later becoming Chief of Staff in 1975, and served in that position until his retirement in 1981.

Political career[]

Mayor of Phoenix[]

In 1981, just three months after his retirement from the Army, Kwiatkowski decided to run for mayor of Phoenix, representing the Republican Party. He budgeted $500,000 towards his campaign, and defeated Ava Shiner, of the Socialist Party. During his first term, he actively focused on improving the city's economy, which was suffering at the time, by diversifying economic activities and increasing real estate developement. He also legalized psilocybin therapy under strict regulations, and comissioned the building of new public parks in undeveloped areas. In 1984, during an economic policymaking session, a member of the opposing party threw a shoe at him, but he dodged it and dismissed his childish antics, convincing many people to support his plan.

In 1985, he was reelected after defeating Democrat candidate Anita Rossi, although it is alleged that he accepted a large campaign donation from the owner of a company linked to drug trafficking. During his second term, Kwiatkowski created an affordable housing project which welcomed hundreds of disadvantaged families. In 1987, he created a state-of-the-art cancer research facility based on a change of location to avoid potentially harmful environmental issues. At the end of the term, he had achieved a record-breaking approval of 100%.

Governor of Arizona[]

In 1989, Kwiatkowski announced that he would run for Governor of Arizona, budgeting $5 million towards his campaign. He defeated Will Licker of the Democratic Party and was elected governor. During his first term, he focused on economic developement and social issues. He also increased tourism by hiring a marketing firm to develop a campaign that highlighted Arizona's landscapes, and hired additional police forces in response to growing crime rates.

In 1993, he ran for reelection and easily defeated Democrat Jack Phelps. Kwiatkowski's approval ratings improved even more during this second term, as he initiliazed a student loan forgiveness program to graduates who served ten years as medical professionals, and after he was accused by the opposition of favouring big business over social welfare, he worked with them in a bipartisan manner to make sure both issues were equally addressed. Though a staunch fiscal conservative, Kwiatkowski was also very pragmatic and worked on all fronts, and his environmental and social policies were universally praised.

Presidency: First Term[]

In 1997, on the Isaac Fartlander show, he announced that he would run for President of the US. He budgeted $300 million in his campaign and entered the race against Brad Borchet, who was representing the Democratic Party. Despite Kwiatkowski's great popularity in the South, voters in more progressive states felt distanced from his campaign, and, ultimately, Borchet won. During the next 4 years, he began raising funds to become president, mainly through sports bets and investments. He also published a book, One Day at a Time, to share his political vision with the American citizens, and it soon became one of the most important contemporary political texts in American history.

In 2001, Kwiatkowski ran again for president, budgeting $1 billion for his campaign, and representing the Republicans. He faced Democrat candidate Elizabeth Dancer and, boosted by the popularity of his new book and the increased funding of the campaign, defeated her and became the 42nd President of the United States. During his inauguration, he announced his objectives on cutting taxes and introducing educational reforms, as well as fostering technological developement and expanding the reach of the Internet. Kwiatkowski has often been labeled as a "progressive conservative"

In his second year, he increased intelligence budget after informations of a potential terrorist threat, and passed a law to make it illegal for parents to give stupid names to their children. In 2003, he offered a Vietnamese politician to rent him some land at a high cost for a military base, but he declined saying that the cost was too high.

Second Term[]

In 2004, Kwiatkowski ran for reelection, defeating Shaniqua Carr of the Fascist Party by a comfortable margin. His second term made him even more popular with the American people, as he got very high approval ratings in practically everything except environment, as his economic policies often had climatic side effects. In 2005, he made the controversial decision to reallocate funds from the education department to cover a deficit in the administration's budget caused by excessive expensing of dining out, drinking booze and receiving body massages. He also attended a Red Cross charity event to show his support for their cause. He left office in 2009, at the age of 90, with an approval rating of 88%.

Post-presidency[]

Following the presidency, Kwiatkowski retired to rural Arizona, where he lived a peaceful life, occasionally appearing on TV interviews and supporting other Republican politicians' campaigns. in 2010, he bought a spa at 256 Provocateur Ave, and supposedly used it as a cover for an independant intelligence agency called "Secret Operations Department", which he led under the codename "Fleming". It allegedly operated for individuals, companies and governments as long as the missions were morally right, although the existance of such an organization has yet to be confirmed.

He died on May 25, 2025, eight days after turning 106, in his villa in Phoenix, and was buried at Arlington National Cemetery with full military honors.

Personal life[]

Kwiatkowski came from a Polish family that was settled in Phoenix after his father emigrated from the Austro-Hungarian Empire in 1907. His grandfather was the notorious scam artist Jakub Kwiatkowski, and his uncle was a police officer in Czechoslovakia called Ignacy Kwiatkowski. He had 6 siblings: Eleanor (1911-2012), who became one of the first female university professors in the US, Josef (1912-1998), who took over his father's firm after his death, Margot (1914-2007), James (1916-1943), who died serving in WW2, and Betty (1917-2012).

In 1945, Kwiatkowski married famous actress Stephanie Saldano at the same church as his parents did, the Cathedral of St. Simon and St. Jude, and she changed her name to Kwiatkowski. The couple had five children: Elizabeth (1946-2018), Edward (b. 1947), William Jr. (1950-1975), Susan (b. 1954) and Mary Grace (b. 1956), and eight grandchildren. He was absolutely devastated when his son William Kwiatkowski Jr. died of autoerotic asphyxiation, and had to rely on alcohol to get through the tragedy.
