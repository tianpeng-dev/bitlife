Arthur Northup (29 December 1711 - 14 July 1797) was an English surgeon who worked at the London General Hospital.

Early Life and Education[]

Arthur Samuel Northup was born in London, on 29 December 1711, to Alexander Northup, a jeweler at Winwood Jewelers, and his wife, Chelsea Northup (née Watkins). In the following years, the Northup couple had two more children, Leah (born 1712) and Jacob Northup (born 1713), before officially separating in early 1715. The divorce agreement granted Arthur's father custody over his eldest.

Around the 1720s, his mother married Andrew Robinson, and had Arthur's half-sister, Audrey Robinson. In 1724, his mother purchased a black-and-white snake from Spanish dealers in the Americas to gift to Arthur and his siblings. They named the snake Grayson, and took care of it until its death from cancer in 1741.

Arthur was sent to a boarding school in Hull, England where he spent most of his adolescent years at. During his time there, he learned the game of Cricket and would often play it with friends in the schoolyard.

After graduating from the boarding school in 1730, Arthur attended the University of St. Andrews and its School of Medicine to pursue his ambitions of becoming a physician. He graduated from the university in 1739, and immediately returned to London in search of work.

One night in 1735, his friend, Benedict Matthews, shaved Arthur's hair off his scalp. Enraged, Arthur attacked him, kicking Matthews in the groin and cracking his jaw. Passersby witnessed the assault and arrested Arthur, before presenting him in court. Fortunately, a defense attorney from Statham & Watson successfully saved him from prison, and Arthur was set free.

Career[]

Returning to London, he applied to the London General Hospital and began working as a surgeon. Arthur gained an above-average reputation for the time and was widely respected by his colleagues. Over the years, he was rewarded with raises to his salary, increasing it from around £88,000 in 1739 to around £531,000 near the end of his tenure in 1775.

Personal Life[]

During his time at the University of St. Andrews, Arthur met a girl called Violet Clarke whom he befriended. In the following months, the pair grew closer and began pursuing a romantic relationship. This culminated in 1737 when they got engaged and were wedded three years later, in 1739.

The following summer, in 1740, Violet gave birth to their first child, Mary-Anne Northup. In 1745, Alice Northup was born. The year after, the couple had David Northup. During an incident in 1760, a court in Birmingham deemed Arthur's cousin, Chester King, and his wife unfit to be parents. They offered custody of their son, Edwin King, to Arthur. After signing an agreement, Arthur and Violet Northup adopted their new son, Edwin Northup (né King). Finally, that same year, Violet gave birth to the couple's last child, Agatha Northup.

His wife passed away on 26 October 1793, aged 82. It left Arthur in immense grief, having been married for 54 years.

Later Life[]

After working for 36 years as a surgeon at the London General Hospital, he retired to enjoy his golden years with his family. In his retirement, he developed type 2 diabetes. He attended his son, David's, funeral after he died from excessive blood loss at the hands of a witch doctor in early 1794.

Death[]

At the age of 85, Arthur was found dead in his home in the early morning of 14 July 1797. He had died in his sleep, having succumbed due to complications from his diabetes. Funeral arrangements were made by his four remaining children. His funeral, conducted at the family home, was attended by his four children, siblings, and extended family. Arthur Northup was buried, beside his wife, on 21 July 1797.
