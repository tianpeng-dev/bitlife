Elizabeth Marjorie Vanderbilt
(November 22, 1935 - March 3, 1998) was an American actress, CEO, and politician. Vanderbilt is known for starring in various types of films, such as "Cinderella" (1957) and "I Am Myth" (1960), with the latter grossing over 200 million dollars ($2,147,480,064 in 2024 dollars). Her impact on cinema and popular culture is profound. She broke barriers for women in the film industry, taking on complex roles that challenged societal norms and inspired future generations of actresses.
Throughout her illustrious career,  Vanderbilt received numerous accolades. She won three Academy awards for Best Actress for her performances in "Cinderella" and "Princesses and Bedchambers" (1962-). Additionally, she was honored with five Golden Globe Awards and received a Lifetime Achievement Award from the American Film Institute. Her contributions to cinema were further recognized with a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame.

Early life
[
]
Elizabeth Marjorie Vanderbilt was born on November 22, 1935 in New York City, New York to Lily Vanderbilt (1915) and Bob Vanderbilt (1905). Her father was an Engineer and her mother was an opera singer, who also pursued a career in acting. From a young age, Elizabeth was surrounded by music, paintings, and performances, which sparked her interest in the arts.
Elizabeth's love for acting began when she was six years old. She would often put on small plays for her family and friends, according to her mother. Her parents, recognizing her passion and talent, enrolled her acting classes at the age of 9. She also participated in school plays and community theater productions.
Career

Career Beginnings
[
]
After graduating from Fuchs-Houston High School in 1953, Vanderbilt set out to look for a talent agent to represent her. In May 1953, she met 31 year old Javier Banner, a talent agent in the Manhattan area, and hired him as her agent. 'The Loyal Tenenbaums', Vanderbilt's critically panned first film, was released a year later and grossed $786,000 against a $1,000,000 dollar budget. Vanderbilt also secured a supporting role in the 1955 film, "Honey, I Sacrificed the Kids." The film was a commercial failure, only managing to gross $780,000 against a budget of $880,000.
Unsatisfactory reviews continued to cause Vanderbilt discomfort. Her 3rd film,  'Rachel Getting Buried', was  deemed a 'laughable attempt at horror' by critics.
Breakthrough, Cinderella and When Time Fades (1956-1959)
[
]
Ready to take her career to the next level, a now 21 year old Vanderbilt auditioned for the lead role of the live action movie
Cinderella
, adapted from the 1950 Disney animated movie. After securing the role, Vanderbilt revealed in a later interview that she had to undergo extensive vocal training before filming commenced. Following the release, 'Cinderella' proved to be a massive success. The film grossed over 220 million dollars (equivalent to 2.53 billion dollars in 2024), and is also credited for catapulting Vanderbilt into stardom.
Personal life
[
]
Vanderbilt met Cristiano Vanderbilt (1939-) in 1960. The two quickly fell in love and welcomed their first child, James Vanderbilt, in 1962. Elizabeth also gave birth to 6 other children, including: Lisa Vanderbilt (1964), Stephanie Vanderbilt (1967), Jonathan Vanderbilt (1971), Paula Vanderbilt (1974), George Vanderbilt (1975), and Carlton Vanderbilt (1979). The pair tied the knot on January 8th, 1965.
While she was celebrating her 28th birthday in 1963, Vanderbilt heard and saw the news of John F. Kennedy's assassination. She later recounted her experience of the tragedy, stating that she had felt a huge loss of innocence and happiness, even calling it her worst birthday ever.
Death
[
]
Vanderbilt died on March 3, 1998, at the age of 62. Her funeral was attended by friends, family, and many of her devoted fans.
