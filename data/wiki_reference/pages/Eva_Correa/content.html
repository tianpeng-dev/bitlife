Eva Correa (March 5, 1977 - August 6, 2002) was a Puerto Rican American based actress and model. She is known for infamously being casted and fired in the 1996 film Mermaid of the Light, an firing that lead to a series of downhill events that earned Correa the honorific names "The Fallen Angel of Hollywood" & "Hollywood's Cautionary Tale". Correa also worked as a model for three years, and appeared as a model in adult magazines from 1996-97.

On July 6, 2002, Correa passed away after an oxycodone overdose on set on set of the television series The Ravine. Correa's highly publicized death led to the cancellation of the series before it could air. In the years following, her tragic career and untimely passing became the subject of widespread speculation. This media attention resulted in her being posthumously referred to as "The Fallen Angel of Hollywood" and "Hollywood's Cautionary Tale."

Life and career[]

1977-1994: Early life, family, and career beginnings[]

Eva Correa was born on March 5, 1977 in San Juan, Puerto Rico. Her mother, Esmeralda Mercado, was a receptionist, and her father, Sergio Correa, was a mail carrier. She had a very close relationship growing up with her half sister, Benita Correa, who was one year older than her. In 1978, her mother married Victor Ayala, who would be the main cause of Eva's struggles in her family life. She revealed that her step-father constantly iced her out and she vied for his acceptance by using her own money earned from working part time on gifts to attempt to buy his love, all to no success.[1] On the flip side, her step brother Edwin Ayala, whom she shared a 13 year age gap with, was very close with her, even once attacking a classmate that was bullying Eva.[1] Correa grew up with a cat from age three, named Lana, who eventually died after three years from falling out of a window.

In 1987, Correa enrolled in acting classes at the age of ten and quickly excelled in the art form, being recognised as the most likely to become a movie star by teachers at her acting school & was known for being amongst the most skilled children.[2] In 1990, Correa worked her first job at 13 years old as a babysitter.[1] Correa was very dedicated to her schoolwork, especially as she reached upper secondary school, juggling both schoolwork, acting classes and working part time, which had an effect on her health as a teenager.

In May 1993, Correa began dating her second ever boyfriend Esteban Albino, with whom she was sexually active with and became pregnant with his child at just age 16, but later had an abortion, citing young age and her hopes and aspirations for her career. She became pregnant with his child again on two separate occasions a year later, choosing to terminate the pregnancy the first time, but kept it the second, however she suffered a first trimester miscarriage. The miscarriage held extra weight on Correa as she had hoped that her unborn child, and her step-brothers unborn child would grow up together.[1]

1995–1996: Initial acting career and blacklisting[]

In April 1995, Correa moved to the United States to pursue her acting career fully. Esteban initially moved with her, however they soon broke up after. Correa made her acting debut doing extra roles in numerous 1995 films, such as Deceased Scriveners Society, The Darkest Afternoon and the experimental film Sweeping Interests. Her role as a 'sex toy part attendant' in the latter caught the eyes of executives Mammoth Studios, who offered her the role of Robyn Ali in the 1996 film Mermaid of the Light. Correa filmed the movie in its entirety, including shoots in Beijing and an on-set disagreement with co-star Alexa Monroe, who brought her cat on set during one of Correa's scenes, leading to the cat disrupting the shoot. The two later laughed about the silly argument that ensued and became close friends after.[3] Promotion for the film garnered mass media attention, especially on October 28, 1995 when the films cast attended the 1995 World Series all dressed in character & went to coffee shops dressed in character.[4] Correa also starred in a series of regional commercials for The Seattle Daily around the time of the movies filming.[5]

In December 1995, Correa had a number of cosmetic surgeries performed, most notably including a face lift that transformed her overall appearance. The surgery lead to Mammoth Studios firing Correa for "looking unrecognisable", and the production company also blacklisted the actor, leading to her being unable to get any roles for the following years. Correa hadn't realised at the time she could've likely sued the company as she was incredibly young at just eighteen.[6] Correa auditioned for ten other roles in 1996 following her firing, being unable to receive a call back or failing the auditions despite her performance and was denied any work as an extra. Correa decided to wait out the blacklisting in hopes of it being only tempoary.[6] Mermaid of the Light was pushed back to re-film and released without Correa.

1996–1997: Adult magazine modelling, relocating[]

In late 1996, Correa posted ads to work as a model for adult magazines as a way to keep afloat as she tried to continue to flourish her career as a 19 year old with no family in the United States. She managed to book work being featured in pages in adult magazines in 1996 and 1997, however she decided to temporarily move back to San Juan, Puerto Rico to reconnect with her family and friends as she had been struggling in the states with no friends or family to lean on at such a dark period in her life. She eventually moved back in the fall of 1997, still unable to get acting work.[1]

1998–2002: Modelling, return to acting and death[]

In early 1998, Correa decided to become a model, appearing in ad campaigns for local dealerships in Seattle. Her first significant campaign came in August when she modelled for Ramos Couture. In 1999, after two years of inactivity in the adult magazine industry, Correa was offered by famous adult model Kaylee Fuchs to appear side by side for Playboy, which gained Correa the most publicity since her 1995 promotional tour for Mermaid of the Light. The magazine shoot became internationally recognised, with friends in Puerto Rico calling Eva about the spread.[6] During her modelling stint, Correa developed both anxiety and an eating disorder due to the massive pressures of the industry, describing it as the lowest point in her life.

In 2000, Correa auditioned for a role in the childrens show Schoolgirl Meets World, where she was casted as the male role of Jalen Moorehead. Correa was also required to jump from a bow of a ship into the ocean for the role. Playing a male character, alongside doing such dangerous stunts made Correa feel like she was being mocked by the creators of the show, but she felt as if she couldn't speak up as this was her only chance back into acting. Schoolgirl Meets World premiered in September 2001 on The WB and was cancelled after just one season due to low ratings. That same year, Correa was cast in the role of Alexandra Clyde in season two of the soap opera Cock of the Chalk. Correa became known amongst the cast and crew for being nice and befriended the entire cast, especially the lead actress Naoki Cuban, whom she had a rocky start with, similar to Alexa Monroe. The cast became Eva's first ever friends she had made in her 6 years of living in the states.

In January 2002, Correa was bought a Los Angeles storybook house by a loyal fan of her adult modelling work, which she admitted creeped her out but she still accepted as she was near broke and homeless. Season two of Cock of the Chalk premiered on NBC in January 2002, introducing Correa's character, with Correa earning over $1 million for her role, however the show was cancelled in December due to low ratings. Correa chose to retire from the adult modelling industry around this time as she felt she was somewhat re-establishing her professional acting career once again.

In April 2002, Correa was offered the role of Schlitz in the crime TV show The Ravine. The series pilot was well received by ABC, who ordered a full season. During production of the first season, Eva Correa was pressured by production crew to take oxycodone during the filming of a scene. As Correa had only just managed to get back into acting following after five years of industry wide blacklisting, she felt as if she had no choice to take the drug. The following morning of her taking the drug on set, she was pronounced dead of a drug overdose in her Los Angeles home. Correa's death became global headline news, with her family immediately taking ABC and the production company to court. The Ravine was cancelled and never got to air after filming twelve episodes, as well as season two of Cock of the Chalk taking a break from airing to respect Correa, with the remainder of the season airing in her honour. The storyline also faced a major shift as filming was not entirely complete and Correa's character had to be written out. A special tribute episode, "In Memory of Eva," aired in Correa’s honor. It was well-received by both fans and critics, acknowledging her contributions to the show and her career.

2002–present: Legal battle and legacy[]

A legal battle ensued from September 2002, with the Correa family alleging negligence and wrongful death against their daughter. They argued that the production crew's pressure on Eva was reckless and amounted to gross negligence, as they failed to ensure the safety of the actress in such a vulnerable situation. ABC and the production company filed a counterclaim asserting that they were not responsible for Correa’s death. They contended that they had provided Correa with the drug under medical supervision, following advice from a certified physician, and that the decision to take the drug was ultimately Correa’s own. Her death caused public outcry and calls for stronger protections for actors in the entertainment industry.

After a lengthy legal battle, ABC reached an out-of-court settlement with the Correa family in 2005, with reports suggesting over $30 million in payouts from ABC. The network also funded a chartity foundation dedicated to substance abuse awareness and actor safety, named in Eva Correa’s memory. Correa’s legacy was honored through the foundation, which provided financial support for actors in recovery and aimed to improve working conditions on sets across Hollywood.

Eva Correa's tragic career and untimely passing became the subject of widespread speculation and interest both online and in media publications. A 2013 MTV documentary was released titled Remembering Eva Correa was released diving into her life and career, and kicked off a new wave of interest in Correa's career in the 2010s internet circuit. With the rise of video essays and social media platform TikTok in the late 2010s and early 2020s, Correa became a frequent topic in pop culture history, especially when discussing the treatment of female celebrities in the 1990s and the 2000s. The growing theorising lead to Correa's best friend of 17 years, from when they were children until her passing, Ariana Rodriguez, to publish a book titled "Justice For Eva" in 2022, where she discussed Eva's life and dove into the most commonly discussed 1995-96 period where Eva was blacklisted and the domino effect that followed. Rodriguez said she wrote the book as the theorising by people online had begun to take a fantasised approach and she wanted people to know just how dark it was in the industry for Eva from the beginning until her death. The book contained a lot of unknown information to the public from Correa herself from the brief period in 1997 when she moved back home.

In May 2024, Eva's family released a then unpublished book written by Eva herself in 2000 that detailed her life in her own words. The book was originally pitched to multiple publishers in 2000 however none were interested in releasing it. The book, released solely online to Eva's website, received critical praise for diving into the more personal attributes of her life like her familial relationships and friendships.
