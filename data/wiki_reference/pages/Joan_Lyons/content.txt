Dame
Elizabeth Joan Lyons
(née
Goldstein
) (29 November 1912 – 6 November 2013) was an Australian politician, judge, philanthropist and matriarch of the
Lyons family
. She served as the 24th
Prime Minister of Australia
from 1987 to 1995. She held office as leader of the
Australian National Party
(ANP), having previously served as
Attorney‑General of Victoria
from 1974 to 1986,
Premier of Victoria
from 1958 to 1970, and
Deputy Premier of Victoria
from 1946 to 1958. She was the first woman ever to hold each of these offices in
Australian history
.

Early life
[
]
Birth and family background
[
]
Elizabeth Joan Goldstein was born on 29 November 1912 at 4 Garden Court in the North End neighbourhood of
Melbourne, Victoria
. She was the youngest of three children born to
Joseph Henry Goldstein Jr.
(1878–1965), then a member of the
Melbourne Common Council
, and
Mary Grace Hallway
(1883–1988), daughter of
Leslie Hallway
(1853-1944), a movie producer. Her brother,
Joseph Henry Goldstein II
(1905–1985), was a trade unionist and entrepreneur, and her sister,
Josephine Grace Menzies
(1908–1999), married
Robert Gordon Menzies
(1900-1978), Prime Minister of Australia from 1939 to 1941 and from 1949 to 1966.
Childhood
[
]
Growing up, Lyons and her siblings “had the normal enjoyments and camaraderie of a small country town.” She began her formal education in 1917 at Jeparit State School, a single‑teacher one‑room school. When she was about eleven, she and her sister were sent to Ballarat to live with their paternal grandmother; their two older brothers were already living there. In 1924 Lyons began attending
Humffray Street State School
in
Bakery Hill
. The following year, at age 13, she ranked first in the state‑wide scholarship examinations. This achievement financed her entire secondary education, which she undertook at private schools, as Victoria did not yet have a system of public secondary schools. In 1927 and 1928 Lyons attended
Grenville College
, a small private school in Ballarat Central. In 1929 she enrolled at
Wesley College
. Lyons was “
not very interested in and certainly incompetent at sport,
” but she excelled academically. In her third and final year at Wesley she won a £40 exhibition for university study, one of twenty‑five awarded by the state government.
University
[
]
In 1931, at age 19, Lyons entered
Melbourne Law School
. She won various prizes, exhibitions and scholarships during her time as a student, graduating with a
Bachelor of Laws
(LL.B.) in 1934 and a
Master of Laws
(LL.M.) in 1936. However, she failed Latin in her first year. One of her prize‑winning essays,
The Rule of Law During the War
, was published as a brochure with an introduction by Harrison Moore, the law school dean. In 1934 Lyons was elected President of the
Student Representatives’ Council
and became editor of the
Melbourne University Magazine
. She contributed both prose and poetry to the magazine, and even wrote a song about “little
Billy Hughes
” for an end‑of‑year revue. Lyons was also President of the
Female Students’ Christian Union
, a founding member of the Historical Society, and a prominent member of the Law Students’ Society, earning a reputation as “
an unusually bright and articulate member of the undergraduate community
.”
Legal career
[
]
After graduating from the
University of Melbourne
in 1934 with first‑class honours in law, Lyons was admitted to the
Victorian Bar
and the
High Court of Australia
. Establishing her own practice in Melbourne, she specialised chiefly in constitutional law, which she had studied under the leading Victorian jurist and future High Court judge
Sir Owen Dixon
. In 1936 Lyons acted for the Amalgamated Society of Engineers in a case that became a landmark authority on the positive reinterpretation of Commonwealth powers over those of the states. The High Court’s verdict raised her profile as a skilled advocate, and she was appointed
King’s Counsel
in 1938.
Political career
[
]
Local politics
[
]
Before entering parliament, Lyons had a distinguished career in public service. She served as
Deputy Secretary of Justice
for Melbourne in 1943. From 1940 to 1942 she worked as a clerk in the
Melbourne City Council
, and in 1944 was appointed
Secretary of Justice
. In 1945 she became
Deputy Lord Mayor of Melbourne
under
Sir Bob Gillard
(1898–1988), and later that year assumed the office of Lord Mayor, serving until 1946.
State politics
[
]
In 1946 Lyons entered state parliament as a member of the
Victorian Legislative Council
for
East Yarra Province
, representing the
Australian National Party
. She championed constitutional democracy, the rule of law, the sanctity of contracts, and the preservation of existing institutions. Suspicious of the Labor Party, she stressed the superiority of free enterprise, except for certain public utilities such as the railways. Her candidacy was nearly derailed when ex‑servicemen attacked her in the press for not having enlisted, but she weathered the crisis. Within weeks she was appointed Minister without Portfolio in Premier
William McPherson’s minority government
. The following year she shifted to the
Legislative Assembly
as
Member for Nunawading
. In 1949 she founded the
Female Young Nationalists
—her party’s female youth wing—and served as its first president. Lyons was
Deputy Premier of Victoria
from 1958 to 1970, and
Attorney‑General
from 1974 to 1986.
Prime Ministership
[
]
On 5 June 1987, aged 76, Joan Lyons became the 24th
Prime Minister of Australia
, making her the oldest person to win a national election until
Joe Biden’s U.S. presidential victory in 2020
at age 78. Her leadership focused on economic reform, social welfare and enhancing Australia’s international standing.
Economic and Social Policies
[
]
Lyons’s government pursued economic modernization, introducing the Goods and Services Tax (GST), reducing tariffs, and promoting privatization. She expanded Medicare and enacted social‑welfare reforms in education, healthcare and housing, and advanced women’s rights initiatives, particularly supporting working women in the public service.
Foreign Policy
[
]
She strengthened trade ties with Asia—especially
China
and
Japan
—while maintaining alliances with the United States and the United Kingdom. Lyons played a key role in establishing the
Asia‑Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC)
forum and took proactive stances on global environmental issues, joining international climate‑change agreements.
Social Reforms and Education
[
]
Domestically, her administration increased university funding, introduced higher‑education loan schemes, and expanded vocational training and access for disadvantaged groups.
Immigration Policy
[
]
Lyons continued policies to attract skilled migrants, with a focus on the
Asia‑Pacific region
, thereby contributing to Australia’s multicultural development.
Second term
[
]
Re-elected in 1991, at almost 80, Lyons oversaw a period of economic transformation. The Australian economy shifted towards more market-oriented policies, including greater deregulation and privatization. The implementation of the GST in 1993 and the focus on free-market policies were central to her economic agenda. The reforms were not without their challenges, but by the end of her tenure, the Australian economy had undergone a significant transformation, with growth rates and employment improving. In 1995, as
Dame
after being appointed by
Queen Elizabeth II
, Joan Lyons stepped down as Prime Minister in 1995 after a long and impactful tenure. She left behind a legacy of economic and social reform, a stronger global standing for Australia, and a commitment to modernizing the country.
Personal life
[
]
On 25 January 1936, Goldstein married
Anthony Harold Lyons
(1910-1988) at
Kew Presbyterian Church
in
Melbourne
. Anthony, son of Commonwealth Liberal member
Anthony Lyons II
(1875-1953), helped them purchase a home in
Howard Street, Kew
, their family residence for 25 years. They had three children:
Anthony IV
(1937–1997),
Joseph “Lewis”
(1939) and
Josephine “Harper”
(born 1940).
Anthony Harold Jr. was born on 14 January 1937 in Hawthorn; he married Marjorie Cook in 1964, had six children and died on 8 September 2010 in Kooyong;
Lewis was born on 12 October 1937, remained unmarried, and died in 2020 in East Melbourne;
Harper, born on 8 December 1938, married diplomat Peter Henderson in 1958; their daughter, Elizabeth Joan, was born in 1959.
Allegations of an affair with
Alfred Fairfax
(1933-2012) during her prime ministership were made by
Mungo MacCallum
but later disputed by
Gerard Henderson
and the
Lyons family
.
Death and Funeral
[
]
Lyons died of a heart attack on 6 November 2013 at her home in
Malvern, Melbourne
.
Queen Elizabeth II
sent condolences: “
I was distressed to hear of the death of Dame Joan Lyons. She was a distinguished Australian whose contribution to her country and the Commonwealth will long be remembered.”
Prime Minister
Julia Gillard
declared, “
All Australians will mourn her passing. Dame Joan leaves an enduring mark on Australian history.
”
She received a state funeral at
Scots’ Church, Melbourne
, on 8 November 2013, attended by more than 100,000 people lining the streets.
Prince Charles
represented the Queen. Former Australian Prime Ministers
Julia Gillard
,
Malcolm Fraser
,
John McEwen
,
John Gorton
,
William McMahon
,
Frank Forde
and
Gough Whitlam
attended, as did former UK Prime Ministers
Tony Blair
and
Gordon Brown
. A private family service followed at
Springvale Crematorium
, concluding with a 19‑gun salute. In December 2013 a memorial service was held at
Westminster Abbey
. Lyons’s and her husband’s ashes were interred in the
Prime Ministers’ Garden
at
Melbourne General Cemetery
. Her estate was valued at approximately AUD 7 billion.
