John Buckles
(September 11, 1923 - June 6, 2024), also known as
JB
, was a United States Army veteran who participated in the Normandy invasion of D-Day in World War II. After completing his service in the military, he went on to pursue multiple professions, and even served in three political offices, including School Board Director from 1945 to 1953, Mayor of Sacramento from 1969 to 1981, and Governor of California from 1981 to 1989. Buckles' prominence as a veteran and later careers were partially tainted by several legal issues, including the 1972 murder of a former friend-turned-enemy of his, of which he was later convicted and sentenced to death, but he managed to overturn the decision by appeal.

Early life
[
]
John was born in Sacramento to Cassandra Buckles (1894 - 1969), a real estate agent. From his recollection, he never met his biological father, who supposedly abandoned him at birth. Given John's birth in the early 1920s, it is largely assumed that his father was a World War I veteran. John was a high-spirited child and had many interests. In middle school, he was a member of the weightlifting, animal rights, environment, poetry, and history clubs. He became the vice-president of the weightlifting club in eighth grade. Despite working out as often as possible, John was physically unfit to play sports during his school years. In high school, he became an actor, joining the drama club, for which he became president in his senior year. He also was a member of the chess, astronomy, and politics clubs. His best friend was
Steven "Steve" Walsh
(1923 - 2005), a photographer for
The Sacramento Chronicle
.
Another friend of John's was Marla Sharabati (1923 - 1972). They met in high school and maintained a decent friendship for many years. On several occasions, Marla would ask John to become her best friend, but he would always politely decline, stating that Steve would always be his best friend, because he had known him longer. Allegedly, this would provoke Marla into believing that John was being a bad friend to her, and they would have disputes later in their lives.
Military career
[
]
Buckles enlisted in the Army in 1941, close to the time of the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor. His platoon members included Robert "Bob" Eastwood,
Buster Brian
(1904 - 1950), Charles Little, Julius Snatch, Luther Wallace, Maxwell "Max" Hole, and Matthew "Matt" Ryder.
D-Day
[
]
In June of 1944, Buckles was among the many soldiers who were to be deployed on Omaha Beach as part of the invasion of Normandy, France. In the days leading up to the attack, he would spend time mingling with his fellow soldiers on his ship on the English Channel, and also write letters to his friends and family back home. On June 6, Buckles stormed the beach with his platoon, dodged as many bullets as possible, and took down several Nazis along the way.

Postwar activities
[
]
After receiving his honorable discharge at the end of the war, Buckles went on to work in many different jobs. Every year or two for around a decade, he was a cashier, receptionist, factory worker, fisherman, taxi driver, police officer, bus driver, truck driver, bartender, porn set janitor, and wedding planner. He used his status as a jack-of-all trades to add diversity to his professional resume, which would help him in important roles.
In 1965, at 42 years old, Buckles enrolled in college, majoring in political science. He had wanted to go to university when he was younger, but was unable to do so due to his wartime service and other factors from the previous years. Since he was "too old" to participate in any extracurricular activities, he opted to have on-campus employment to stay active while studying, and thus took up an admissions assistant job. Buckles graduated from college in 1969.
Politics
[
]
Buckles' first political office was of the California School Board of Education, in which he started serving as director in 1945. He had beaten his opponent, Maurice Price (1875 - c. 1955). In 1946, Buckles refused a parent's $1,100 bribe to ensure that her child make the school's volleyball team. He then made a speech to spread awareness about the importance of education. In 1947, Buckles asked his personal driver to help mediate a fight between him and his wife after she accused him of flirting with a fellow politician at that year's masquerade ball. In 1948, Buckles implemented a schedule in his district that had schools operating for ten hours a day, Monday through Thursday.
At the end of his first term in 1949, he ran for reelection against Meixiu Hsiao (1905 - c. 1990s). Buckles defeated Hsiao and won the election. In 1950, he gained positive publicity when he participated in a beach cleanup event. That same year, he staged an enthusiastic rally, and again the following year. In 1952, a homeless person was caught trying to steal food at Buckles' fundraising event. Instead of turning him in to the authorities, Buckles invited the homeless man to address the philanthropic gathering. He completed his second and final term as school board director in 1953.
In 1969, after graduating from university, Buckles ran for mayor.
Personal life
[
]
Buckles met Farah Groper (1923 - 2025) at the age of seven in 1930. They started going out as a couple in 1932 and married in 1944 (after D-Day) while he was on leave. Buckles returned to service the day after the wedding. He and Groper had three children together, including
Clinton
(born 1948), Mercedes (1950 - 2022), and Lee (born 1958). Clinton is a retired truck driver and Vietnam War veteran, Mercedes was a real estate agent, and Lee is a retired insurance agent. Groper allegedly cheated on Buckles multiple times, and Buckles often contemplated divorce, but they ultimately stayed together until Buckles' death.
John shared the same last name as World War I veteran and supercentenarian Frank Buckles. Despite this, there is a lack of evidence suggesting the two were related.
Legal issues
[
]
Buckles' post-military life was partially defined by trouble with the law. In 1956, he was arrested for drug possession when throwing a house party, at which police found liquid acid. He was later acquitted on the charges due to a lack of hard evidence linking him to the crime. Meanwhile, Sharabati and Buckles' friendship was still rough. In 1958, he unsuccessfully tried to stage an intervention for her alcohol addiction. Enraged, she charged at him, inverting his mouth. Buckles sued Sharabati for $6,000. He lost the case. A year later, Sharabati officially dissolved their friendship and declared him her enemy. Buckles' tried many times to get back on Marla's good side, but to no avail. This made Buckles snap, and he decided that Sharabati and her antics "ought to be dealt with."
In 1972, Buckles killed Sharabati by performing a drive-by shooting on her. He managed to get away with it for nearly twenty years. In 1991, Buckles was arrested for Sharabati's murder. He unsuccessfully tried to run from the cops, adding felony escape to the charges. Unable to afford an attorney, he hired a public defender for his case. In his defense, Buckles argued that he killed Sharabati in self-defense, because she had antagonized him in many different ways and might have posed as a threat to him and his family. At the conclusion of his trial, he was found guilty of Marla's murder and sentenced to death by lethal injection. His execution was scheduled for his 70th birthday in 1993. Shortly after his conviction, however, Buckles hired Bradford & Associates to appeal the decision. It went in his favor, and he was released from prison. Afterwards, Bradford & Associates were met with much media scrutiny, and the public criticized the firm for "helping a cold-blooded murderer walk free." In later years, the story would also be compared to the O. J. Simpson murder case, given potential similarities. Buckles' political and diplomatic immunity had also been permanently revoked after his initial conviction.
Buckles was arrested in 1998 for attacking a UFC fighter over an argument they instigated upon each other. He headbutted his face and cracked him in the jaw. The specific fighter is largely disputed as either Frank Shamrock or Pat Miletich, both of whom were prominent fighters at the time. Buckles was charged with the assault, and he pled not guilty and claimed self-defense because the fighter instigated the incident by calling him a coward. He was acquitted on the charges. Buckles and Shamrock (or Miletich) continued their beef for a while until they had a personal boxing match to settle the dispute once and for all. After the match, which ended in a relatively close draw, they settled their differences and became friends.
Death
[
]
Buckles died on June 6, 2024, the 80th anniversary of D-Day, aged 100. He is survived by his two sons and any potential grandchildren. He was one of the last surviving D-Day veterans, and possibly one of the very last from his year of birth. While most people mourned his death, others, who still resented him for Sharabati's murder, celebrated his passing.
