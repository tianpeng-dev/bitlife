Piotr Jablonski
(December 16, 1946 - April 5, 2024) was a Polish-American baseball player who was well renowned for playing with the New York Bronx. Not only was he renowned for playing baseball, but also known to be a family man, and was well-loved by his family. Born in Poznan, Poland Jablonski emigrated to the United States to escape the communist government. He joined the San Diego Papals in 1964, and rose to stardom by playing with the New York Bronx. With three championship wins in 1974, 1976, and 1981; he is regarded as one of the greatest baseball players of all time.

Early Life
[
]
Piotr Jablonski was born on December 16, 1946 in Poznan, Poland to Pawel Jablonski (1918-1984), a nude art model, and Lidia Rutkowski (1920-2005), a trucker. 3 years later in 1949, his parents had his younger sister Emilia (b. 1949). In 1951, Jablonski, his parents, and his little sister all emigrated to the United States to escape the communist government of Poland. Jablonski was upset about the move, but later learned to love the United States. The family settled in Cincinnati, Ohio. He would never return to Poland.
Jablonski began attending Uranus Elementary School in 1952. In 1954, his parents had his younger sister Rita (b. 1954). That same year, he also befriended Zhenis Omarov (b. 1946) who, like him was also an immigrant. During his school years, he was a studious student, but also outgoing and quite athletic. In 1957, he began middle school at Glacier Middle School. His parents had his little sister Mariah (1960-1989) in 1960. In 1964, Jablonski graduated from Orchid High School.
Baseball Career
[
]
Jablonski was quite an athletic kid during his childhood. In 1960, he joined his high school's baseball team, and later in 1959, he was promoted to team captain. Upon graduating from high school, he made himself eligible for the professional baseball draft. Jablonski was drafted by the San Diego Papals, and offered a 2 year, $1.2 million contract to play for them. After attempting to negotiate a higher payout, but was rejected; Jablonski joined the San Diego Papals as their new pitcher. A journalist for a local newspaper saw this as newsworthy, and wrote an article about this. The article was a major success, which resulted in Jablonski being propelled to instant stardom.
Early Career with the New York Papals (1964-1970)
[
]
Upon joing the Papals, Jablonski began training rigorously. He would wake up at 4 AM everyday, to run several miles, and kept a strict diet regimine. This resulted in him quickly attaining respect from his colleagues, and it increased his value as a baseball player. After his contract expired in 1966, he was offered a 4-year, $38 million contract to continue playing with the Papals. Jablonski tried to negotiate for more money, but was rejected. Nevertheless, he still accepted the contract.
During his time with the Papals, Jablonski was seen as the one who was carrying the team; while he was an exceptional player, the rest of his team wasn't so good. During his time with them, the Papals highest finish in the baseball league was in 7th place. In 1970, Jablonski was offered a $58.8 million contract to continue playing with the Papals for another 6 years. Jablonski accepted the contract, but immediately requested a trade to the New York Bronx, a more higher ranked team than the Papals. His trade was arranged, and Jablonski said goodbye to the Papals.
Journey to the Championship Finals (1970-1981)
[
]
After being traded to the Bronx, Jablonski continued training, and wroking hard. His fame increased drastically, as many were amazed at his devotion to baseball. In an interview with the New York Times, Jablonski said, "I will never retire from baseball, and will continue playing into my old age, unless I carry my team to the Baseball Championship Finals, and win." His wish later came true in 1974, when Jablonski led the Bronx to the Championship Finals and won. For his win, he was offered a championship ring, which he gracefully accepted.
It is rumoured that also in 1974, a fan of Jablonski's poured soda down his back in front of him as a show of his undying love for Jablonski. Jablonski then allegedly attacked the fan by biting his neck, and punching him, leaving the fan with a bruised brain. The fan's injuries were so severe that he later died in hospital. No evidence exists to prove this rumour, and while Jablonski himself never addressed this rumour, his publicist said in a 2001 interview that Jablonski was too kind to have assaulted and killed a fan of his. However, it is a well known fact that whenever Jablonski got really mad (which was very rarely), he could have a monstrous temper.
Jablonski won a second Baseball Championship in 1976, also winning the Championship MVP award for being the most valuable player on his team that year. He won a third Baseball Championship in 1981, securing his place as one of the greatest players in American Baseball history. Follwing this win, he was released by the New York Bronx, who believed that his time was up. However, Jablonski's enduring popularity prompted the Washington Corruption, who'd wanted to get their hands on him, to offer him a 6-year, $71.4 million contract to play for them. Jablonski accepted the contract an got to work as a pitcher for the Corruption. Despite it's name, studies indicate that the Washington Corruption was one of the least corrupt baseball teams.
Time with the Washington Corruption (1981-1987)
[
]
During his time with the Corruption, Jablonski worked hard at training for baseball. He also received some dental implants in 1983 to replace his teeth, as over the course of his career he'd lost 21 out his his 32 teeth due to injuries and balls hitting him in the mouth. In 1984, Jablonski's father Pawel died at the age of 66 from old age. Jablonski was devastated, as his father had been a kind of hero for him, and was his number one supporter. Jablonski arranged a fitting, yet fancy funeral for his father, and gave a tearful eulogy that was praised by many, and for years after, other people giving speeches would reference Jablonski's eulogy.
Despite his best efforts, the highest the Corruption reached in the baseball league was second place. Jablonski said in an interview with the Associated Press, "I believe that winning is not everything in sport. But it's still a whole damn lot, and I'd really like to win just one more time." His wish never came true, and in 1987, the Corruption released him at the end of his contract, believing that Jablonski had lost his spark. However, the Houston Buzzers saw an opportunity, and offered him a $33.2 million contract to play with them. Jablonski accepted, and got to work as their new pitcher.
Late Career with the Houston Buzzers (1987-1991)
[
]
Jablonski also put his time into playing for the Buzzers, but not as much as before, as he believed that his baseball career was winding down. During his later career, he was less recognized for his playing ability, and more for his skill. He carried the Buzzers, but the highest the team got while he was with them was 6th place. In 1991, Jablonski stirred debate and minor controversy, when he chose to kneel during the playing of the US National Anthem during several of his games. Jablonski said that he was protesting the unfair treatment of minority groups in the US. While Jablonski was not a political person, and rarely expressed opinions on certain issues, many believe that he was supportive of equality. In 1991, Jablonski was released from the Houston Buzzers at the end of his contract. Jablonski announced the end of his career, saying that it had been a journey.
Personal Life
[
]
Jablonski was very close with his family, and he loved them all very dearly. He was the oldest of four children, and had three younger sisters: Emilia (b. 1949), Rita (b. 1954), and Mariah (1960-1989). In 1989, Mariah was killed after she was accidentally hit by a golf ball on the head. Jablonski was devastated, and attended her funeral, and gave a tearful eulogy.
Jablonski kept a close relationship with his parents, especially his father. When Jablonski's father died in 1984, he planned a lavish funeral for him, and gave a tearful eulogy at it. Jablonski kept a close relationship with his mother, but later became cold to her after in 1985, she married Javier Snickles (1916-1992), a retired internal auditor with two children: Genesis Dingleberry (1934-2019) and Ella Snickles (1950-2014). Jablonski was antagonistic towards his new stepfather, and called him "Daddy Snickles" to spite him. He believed that his mother had betrayed his father by marrying someone else. Jablonski kept a friendly relationship with his stepsisters though. Jablonski still honoured his mother by attending her funeral in 2005.
Jablonski took a girl named Riley Kent to the school dance in 1958, and later became best friends with her. In 1960, he asked her out and started dating her. A year later, his parents disapproved of Riley, saying that the differences between their cultures were too great. Jablonski then broke up with her. In 1966, he began dating Tina White, and dated her for two years before breaking up with her and started dating Diana Wang. In 1970, Jablonski began dating his future wife Stephanie Cross (b. 1949). A year later he proposed to her, and she accepted. The two married in 1972, and went to Havana for their honeymoon. Stephanie decided to hyphenate her last name, becoming Stephanie Cross-Jablonski.
Jablonski and Stephanie had 6 children: Nikola (b. 1974), Heather (b. 1976), Mikiyah (b. 1979), Ashley (b. 1981), Julius, and Piotr Jr. (b. 1985). Jablonski was very close to all of his children, and was a loving and supportive dad. He also was very close to his wife, and the two remained married until Jablonski's death in 2024. Jablonski was mostly faithful to his wife, but there were a few occasions where he did cheat on her.
Jablonski had many friends throughout his life, but his childhood friend Zhenis made him his enemy in 1964. The two remained rivals for the rest of Jablonski's life. Accodring to Jablonski, he believed that Zhenis was jealous of his international fame as a baseball player.
Later Life
[
]
Jablonski spent the rest of his life with his family, though he did publish a few memoirs here and there, and appeared in talk shows and commercials. Eventually, he faded into obscurity. He attended his stepfather's funeral in 1992, but was reported to have been doing a crossword puzzle during the service. He also paid his respects at his mother's funeral in 2005. In 2014, Jablonski's stepsister Ella died after eating improperly prepared pufferfish. Jablonski paid his respects at her funeral. He also paid his respects at the funeral of his stepsister Genesis in 2019. Jablonski also attended his children's weddings, and paid for their college education. He believed college education to be important, as he'd never gone to college himself.
Death
[
]
Piotr Jablonski died in his sleep in the early hours of April 5, 2024. He was 77 years old. His funeral was held on April 30, 2024 and his wife, his sisters Emilia and Rita, and his six children all attended his funeral. At the time of his death, he was worth $100,243,960.
