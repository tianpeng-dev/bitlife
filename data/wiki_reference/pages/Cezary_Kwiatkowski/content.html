Cezary Kwiatkowski Esq. (June 12, 1884 - April 10, 1971) was a Polish-Czech-American criminal defense attorney, legal scholar, gambler and investor. He is best known for being the father of US president William Kwiatkowski.

Early life and education[]

Cezary was born in Cracow, Austrian Poland, to the notorious scam artist Jakub Kwiatkowski (1856-1888), and Katarzyna Kalinowski (1857-1932), an accountant. He had an older brother, Ignacy (1882-1955), who would later become a lieutenant in the Imperial-Royal Police.

In 1886, his family moved to Ostrava, in the Kingdom of Bohemia (also part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire), fleeing authorities. When he was only 4 years old, his father was killed during a worker's protest in Leitmeritz (modern-day Litoměřice) while serving in the Austro-Hungarian Army. This was a major trauma for him and would later define his life.

In 1890, Cezary was enrolled in St. Tuan's Primary School, where he excelled at grammar and languages. In 1899, he started secondary school at Ostrava Secondary School and joined the school's soccer team, of which he would become captain 3 years later.

He entered Charles University in Prague in 1903 and began a degree in Criminal Justice. He also joined the university's soccer team, becoming captain in 1906. During his university years he was top of his class, always getting the highest marks in jurisprudence, criminal procedure, and legal history. His professors noted his incredible ability to solve complex legal scenarios and learning by heart all sorts of cases and rulings. He contributed articles to the legal journal Právnický Obzor, and was a member of the Debate Club.

In 1907, he graduated cum laude and was offered to become a professional soccer player (at the time a poorly paid job), but, encouraged by his professors, he decided to go to law school in the United States. He applied for a scholarship in Harvard Law School through the Sheldon Fellowship and it was awarded to him in full. There, he had a 100% success rate at mock trials by playing both defense and prosecution. His paper “On the Limits of Retributive Justice” won the Williston Prize, and his essay on contract law was published in the Harvard Law Review, which was pretty rare for a foreign student at the time.

Legal career[]

Cezary Kwiatkowski graduated from Law School in 1910, obtaining his Juris Doctor (JD) diploma. In 1911, he was admitted to the Arizona State Bar, and shortly after hired by the prestigious law firm Willikers & Associates, based in Phoenix, Arizona. In 1912, he won $4 million after a series of successful sports bets (in baseball, boxing and horse races, mainly), and immediately began investing his money in several companies and corporate bonds.

In 1922, he was promoted to Partner, so his firm was renamed Willikers, Kwiatkowski & Associates. By the mid-1920s, he had gained a reputation as a top criminal defense lawyer in Phoenix. His legal practice flourished during the Prohibition era, as he became the go-to attorney for bootleggers, gangsters, and other individuals involved in organized crime. His most famous case was State v. Burke, where he defended his friend Thomas Burke, a wealthy businessman accused of ordering the murder of a rival. Despite overwhelming evidence against his client, Kwiatkowski dismantled the prosecution’s case, and his client was miraculously acquitted. This made him one of the most famous lawyers in the United States at the time. He was also an accomplished legal scholar, publishing several articles that reached wide circulation in the US.

In 1939, after the outbreak of WW2, he publicly dennounced Hitler's invasion of Poland and financially supported the Polish government in exile. After the war ended in 1945, he briefly returned to Ostrava to visit his brother Ignacy, who had been fighting with the Czech partisans. In 1951 he fired his financial advisor and liquidated his investment portfolio (worth $16 million at the time) for unknown reasons.

He retired from legal practice in 1954 and dedicated the rest of his life to writing articles and giving speeches about several topics, mainly about prison reforms.

Personal life[]

Cezary had a very close relationship with his mother and brother, often sending them gifts and writing letters to them. In 1904, his mother married Hans Fiala, an Austrian military officer, of whom Cezary would become very fond of. He was devastated when he heard of his death in 1915, during the Battle of Gorlice-Tarnów of WW1. He was also a good friend of chemical engineer Peter Hajek and fellow lawyer Matthew Morris.

At the age of 18, he hooked up with several random women, leading him to briefly contract gonhorrea and impregnate one of them, Lucie Vesely. Luckily, she got a second-trimester miscarriage, but Kwiatkowski later confessed this was the biggest scare of his life.

In 1908, during his time at Harvard Law School he began dating a nursing school student called Mary Grace Carter. In 1910, they married at the Cathedral of St. Simon and St. Jude in Phoenix, and Mary Grace changed her last name to Kwiatowski. They remained married until Cezary's death in 1971, and had six children: Eleanor (1911-2012), Josef (1912-1998), Margot (1914-2007), James (1916-1943), Betty (1917-2012) and William (1919-2025).

He died in 1971, aged 87, and his funeral was attended by his wife and loving children. He is buried in St. Francis Catholic Cemetery along with Mary Grace.
