Ignacy Kwiatkowski (December 2, 1882 - March 11, 1955) was a Polish-Czech police officer, World War 1 veteran and Czech resistance fighter. He was the older brother of prominent lawyer Cezary Kwiatkowski.

Life and career[]

Cezary was born in Cracow, Austrian Poland, to the notorious scam artist Jakub Kwiatkowski (1856-1888), and Katarzyna Kalinowski (1857-1932), an accountant. His family moved to Ostrava, Kingdom of Bohemia, fleeing authorities, and in 1888, his father was killed serving in the Austro-Hungarian army.

In 1900, he joined the Austrian Imperial-Royal Police as a cadet. In 1915, he was promoted to Inspector. In 1916, he volunteered for the Austro-Hungarian Army and joined the 10th Imperial-Royal Landwehr Infantry Regiment. He served as a Zugsführer (Platoon Leader) and distinguished himself in the battles of Mount Ortigara and Caporetto. In 1918, he finished the war as a Feldwebel (or Sergeant Major) and returned to his duties as a police officer in the newly formed First Czechoslovak Republic.

In 1925, he was appointed Chief of Police. In March 1939, the Nazis occupied Czechoslovakia, turning it into the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia, and Ignacy was forced to swear the loyalty oath to Hitler and become a member of the NSDAP. Since then, he became chief of the Sicherheitspolizei (SiPo) in Ostrava. A model officer on the outside, he secretly joined the Czech resistance. Using his position, he saved Jewish families by falsifying documents, fed information to resistance cells and Allied intelligence, and destroyed case files. In 1945, he was captured by Soviet forces but promptly released.

After WW2, he retired from the police and lived peacefully until he contracted lupus in 1954 and died the following year.
